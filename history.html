Early History

Leith Walk was an ill-defined road and probably used only by groups of well-armed companions, or those who carried nothing worth losing to footpads.

Previous Next

The walk was a vague track, down the hill to Leith, keeping to the west of the Greenside burn and skirting the sandy mounds through which innumerable bubbling springs found their way towards the sea. To his east lay the lands of Restalrig, whose laird, Sir Robert Logan, had sold the right to use all paths and passages on his land to the town in 1398. Away to the west stood the Peel tower on the ridge, the Pilrig, owned until her death in 1463 by Mary of Gueldres, widow of James II.

In a time, James II granted the town a recreation ground at Greenside in 1456. This was used for jousting and, until the Reformation, for plays like the Satire of the Three Estates. Tradition has it that Mary Queen of Scots first saw Bothwell there, as he rode his charger down the precipitous hill side to the Tournament.

It was a dangerous journey. An array of human corpses, swinging from the gallows set on top of a low mound by the road was an experience calculated by the good burgesses of Edinburgh to awe the most hardened man.

Previous Next

In 1623, Pilrig was owned by Gilbert Kirkwood, and Edinburgh goldsmith, who had bought it from the Moneypenny family. He and his wife, Margaret Foulis, built a house in the Dutch style. They planted two rows of beech and elm trees ending at a simple fence and some broken ground along what later became Leith Walk. There was no avenue between the trees, and no entrance gate, but at either side was a little house. At Greenside the Town had let out the Playfield as arable land and pasture.

In 1650, General David Leslie formulated his plan for the defense of Edinburgh against the army of Oliver Cromwell. Leslie decided to have two deep trenches dug all the way from the end of the Calton Hill to Leith. The earth thrown up by the digging formed a rampart to the west of the trenches, more or less on the line of Leith Walk.

In 1695, the Town Council recorded the payment of 60 scots pounds yearly to James Lauder, gardener, Burgess of Edinburgh, “to be keeper and overseer of the walk betwix Edinburgh and Leith, for laying of Channell (gravel) thereon”. George Gigo had been laying gravel on the walk before. All wheeled traffic was banned from using the walk. According to Robert Chambers: “it was about twenty feet wide and it was not completely straight. It skirted the infamous Gallow-lee where witches were strangled and burnt, and Covenanters hanged and beheaded, and wound past houses and a fairly large pond.”

Nursery Gardens and The Botanic Garden

Previous Next

By the time, Leith walk was green and sorrounded by nursery gardens. In 1764, Professor John Hope purchased thirteen acres of land, with the trees and growing timer thereon, on the west side of Leith Walk at Shurbhill, for the new Botanick Gardens. The first head gardener was John Williamson, for whom a neat two storeyed house was built at the entrance gates. The house was still there until 2009, were they moved it to the Botanical Garden in Inverleith. The botanical garden was moved in 1818. It was from Shurbhill that the tea plant seedlings were sent to Blantyre to start the tea industry in Africa.

There are references of other gardening firms – Methven’s, Dod’s, Reid’s, specialized in Camellias, and to James Grieve of Pilrig Street, after whom the well-known apple is named.

Nowadays, Leith Walk is no longer the green garden it used to be and mostly buildings shape its ways.

Early nineteenth century

In about 1780 James Craig, architect of the first New Town, had prepared drawings for straightening and widening the Walk. With his work, a new road city and port were linked and the carts and carriages began to bowl in Leith Walk. At the end of the Napoleonic War, then, Leith Walk was an established carriageway, with good houses at the top; cheaper tenements in Greenside, where many of the inhabitants had workshops and forges; a sprinkling of timber-yards, marble workshops, foundries flint and crownglass-works and manufactories further down, and among the remaining old houses still a number of thriving market-gardens.

Thomas Carlyle

Middlefield was occupied in 1793 by a paper-stainer named Macmillan who advertised the upper flats to let as country lodgins. Thomas Carlyle, an Scottish historian and essayist, took rooms there to escape the noise of mason-work in the New Town. but he wrote to his mother that the barking dog kept him awake at night. Thomas Carlyle’s rooms were in Moray Street, changed later to Spey Street because of postal confusion. Only the lower front walls of the Spey Street tenements now remain.

Thomas experienced in Leith Walk a striking spiritual rebirth which he describes in his work Sartor Resartus (Latin: “The Tailor Re-tailored”). It is humorous essay about the philosophy, the symbolism, and the influence of clothes. He declares that, while most of Sartor Resartus is mere symbolical myth, the sudden spiritual awakening of the imaginary Teufelsdrockh, a character of his essay, happened literally to himself one day in Leith Walk in 1821

Another anecdote of his and according David Masson, "For some purpose or other, he was going down Leith Walk, the long street of houses, stone-yards, and gaps of vacant space, which leads from Edin- burgh to its sea-port of Leith. In front of him, and also walking towards Leith, was a solid, quiet-looking countryman. They had not gone far from Edinburgh when there ad\anced to them from the opposite direction a sailor, so drunk that he needed the whole breadth of the footpath to himself. Taking some umbrage at the countryman, the sailor came to a stop, and addressed him suddenly, “ Go to H “ looking him full in the face. “ ‘Od, man, I’m gaun to Leith,” said the countryman, as if merely pleading a previous engagement, and walked on, Carlyle following him and evading the sailor."

Definitely, any visitor or inhabitant of Leith Walk may not have a spiritual awakening or a hallucination, but the encounter with different characters alienated by alcohol or other substances is a very common event in our beloved walk even in our days, with the particularity that most people would react like the countryman, as if such events were part of the daily life of Leith Walk.

Robert Chambers

Robert Chambers, who with his brother William was an author, bookseller and publisher in Pilrig Street. In 1818, Robert began a career as a bookseller. He had no apprenticeship; he also had no money to purchase stock. However, William gave him all the advice he could and encouraged him to use the family’s tattered collection of books left over from happier and more prosperous times. Robert took a small shop on Leith Walk opposite Pilrig Avenue and began to sell his books.”

I consider important and even illustrative the descriptions and stories written by Robert Chambers:

“It was a broad kind of boulevard, stretching nearly a mile length, constantly used by merchants, clerks, strangers and sea-faring people. In the early years of the present century, it was the daily resort of a multitude of odd-looking dependents on public charity – such as old bling fiddlers seated by the wayside; sailors deficient in an arm or a leg, with long queues hanging down their backs- always singing ballads about sea-fights; and cripples of various sort. Besides its hosts of mendicants, the Walk was garnished with small shops for the sale of shells, corals and other foreign curiosities. It was also furnished with a number of petty public-houses.”

Seems to be that the same spirit of the street prevails in our times still, in which a wide variety of characters populate the street, those you can hear screaming until very late night; all these among the many coffee shops, restaurants, pubs and other kind of shops where one can find a diversity of many curisities, furniture and clothes.

Robert Chambers declares in his “Traditions of Edinburgh”, 1824: "Of all the streets in Edinburgh or Leith, the Walk in former times was certainly the street for boys and girls. From top to bottom, it was a scene of wonders and enjoyments peculiarly devoted to children. Besides the panoramas and caravan-shows, which were comparatively transient spectacles, there were several shows upon Leith Walk, which might be considered as regular fixtures and part of the country-cousin sights of Edinburgh. Who can forget the waxworks of 'Mrs Sands, widow of the late G. Sands,' which occupied a laigh shop opposite to the present Haddington Place, and at the door of which, besides various parrots and sundry birds of Paradise, sat the wax figure of a little man in the dress of a French courtier of the ancien régime, reading one eternal copy of the Edinburgh Advertiser. The very outsides of these wonder- shops was an immense treat; all along the Walk it was one delicious scene of squirrels hung out at doors, and monkeys dressed like soldiers and sailors, with holes behind where their tails came through. Even the half-penniless boy might here get his appetite for wonders to some extent gratified."

Chambers continues: "Besides being of old the chosen place for shows, Leith Walk was the Rialto for objects. This word requires explanation. It is applied by the people of Scotland to persons who have been born with or overtaken by some miserable personal evil. From one end to the other, Leith Walk was garrisoned by poor creatures under these circumstances, who, from handbarrows, wheelbarrows, or iron legs, if peradventure they possessed such adjuncts, entreated the passengers for charity - some by voices of song, some by speech, some by driddling, as Burns calls it, on fiddles or grinding on hand-organs - indeed, a complete continuous ambuscade against the pocket. Shows and objects have now alike vanished from Leith Walk. It is now a plain street, composed of little shops of the usual suburban appearance, and characterised by nothing peculiar, except perhaps a certain air of pretension, which is in some cases abundantly ludicrous."

Visit of His Majesty George the Fourth to Scotland

According to Gazeteers of Scotland, 1828 - W. & A.K. Johnston Limited: "One of the most remarkable of the recent events in the history of Edinburgh, and in our national annals, is the visit of His Majesty George the Fourth to Scotland, and honouring the Palace of his ancestors with his presence. On the 14th August 1822, the Royal George, having His Majesty on board, anchored in Leith Roads. While here His Majesty received the melancholy intelligence of the death of the Marquis of Londonderry."

"The weather being unfavourable His Majesty did not land till the 15th about noon. He was dressed in an Admiral's uniform, with a Thistle and sprig of Heath on his hat; and a superb St Andrew's Cross, presented to him by Sir Walter Scott, in name of the Ladies of Edinburgh."

"This evening the town of Leith was most superbly illuminated. The procession to Edinburgh by Leith Walk was magnificent; and at Gayfield Place His Majesty was received by the Lord Provost and Magistrates, who, at a temporary barrier, delivered to him the Keys of the City."

Mid nineteenth century

New buildings were being built along the west side of the Walk for the occupation by the working classes, mechanics and respectable families.

On the east side the land was still mainly used by nursery gardens down to Smith’s Place. This had been built by an enterprising General Marchant of Leith, Robert Lindsay, who had a great interest in chemistry. He has built a small laboratory behind his house at the end of Smith’s Place – an elegant house which is still the headquarters of a firm of Pharmaceutical Chemist today.

The original Lindsay & Gilmour was established in Edinburgh by Robert Lindsay in 1826 and has been trading from the same site in Elm Row at the top of Leith Walk ever since, making it one of the oldest established pharmacies in Scotland

In 1930 John Raimes purchases Smiths Place House, off Leith Walk in 1834, then buys the land behind the house to build warehouses and laboratories.

Number 2 Smith’s Place was occupied in 1833 by John Crabbie, who made ginger-wine, still produces in Leith today.

The son of Robert Lindsay, Tom, attended the High School from 1817-20 where he became a firm friend of James Nasmyth, son of the painter Alexander Nasmyth, who lived at 47 York Place. Tom used to raise a flag on his father’s house as a signal to James to run down Leith Walk to share in experiments conducted by his father.

Nasmyth's six daughters all became notable artists. His daughters were Jane, Barbara, Margaret, Elizabeth, Anne and Charlotte. His eldest son, Patrick Nasmyth, studied under his father, then went to London and attracted attention as a landscapist.

Another son, James Nasmyth, and the one who I am refering here, was a Scottish engineer and inventor famous for his development of the steam hammer. He retired at the age of 48, and moved to Penshurst, Kent where he developed his hobbies of astronomy and photography. When he was young, he built a prizewinning stream carriage at Anderson’s Foundry, located as well in Leith Walk.

Nasmyth, is his autobriography, declares about Greenside:

“It happened that close to the Calton Hill, in the valley at the northern side, there were many workshops where interesting trades were carried on; there were coppersmiths, tinsmiths, brassfounders, goldbeaters, and blacksmiths. Their shops were all arranged in a busy group at the foot of the hill... their workshops were open to the inspection of passers-by. Little boys looked in and saw the men at work amidst the blaze of fires and the beating of hammers... One of my greatest enjoyments when a child was in going out with the servants to the Calton, and wait while the “claes” bleached in the sun on the grassy slopes of the hill. The air was bright and fresh and pure. The lasses regarded these occasions as a sort of holiday. One or two of the children usually accompanied them. They sat together, and the servants told us their auld-warld stories; common enough in those days, but which have now, in a measure been forgotten... They ran after butterflies and the bumbees, and made acquaintance in a small way with the beauties of nature. Then the servants opened their baskets of provisions, and we had a delightful picnic. Though I am noiting about seventy years w wrafter the date of these events, I can almost believe that I am enjoying the delightful perfume of the wild thyme and the fragant plants and flowers, wafted around me by the warm breezes of the Calton hillside”

As we can see from the pictures above, Greenside Place is far away from what Nasmyth used to enjoy in his childhood. Any of the workshops, butterflies, bumbees, picnics and flowers seem to be a thing of the past, giving way to concrete, brick and the cold, unloving architecture that characterises our days.

Robert Stevenson

Robert Stevenson was a Scottish civil engineer and famed designer and builder of lighthouses.

Robert Stevenson, grandfather of Robert Louis Stevenson, moved in 1798 to the then newly built 2 Baxter’s Place at the head of Leith Walk. Stevenson lived on Baxter’s Place at the head of Leith Walk for most of his later years, moving from no.2 to the far larger no.1 around 1820.

The building was used as an office from 1985 to 2015 and was named "Robert Stevenson House" in his honor. The name was removed when the building was converted into a Marriott Hotel in 2017.

He had spent the war years designing and supervising the erection of lighthouses, round the coasts, and had built up a thriving engineering practice from his home at Baxter’s place.

James Dove, who had a workshop down the lane in Greenside was a maker of pumps and fire-engines, and parts he made for Stevenson’s lights used to be laid out there for his inspection.

Previous Next

Stevenson died at 1 Baxter’s Place on 12 July 1850 in Edinburgh. Stevenson made drawings for the complete regrading of Leith Walk and this work was carried out by the city, though under the Leith Walk Trust a toll was imposed at the old Gallowlee in 1809 to help to pay for it.

Robert Chambers, in Gazeteers of Scotland - 1838, declares: "While the above mentioned improvements were in the course of execution, the extension of the city to the east of the New Town went on very slowly, and for many years the road to Leith by Leith Walk remained in a wretched condition. Active measures were, however, adopted to remodel " the Walk," which has been done at a prodigious expense, liquidated by a toll-bar. This way, which is altogether paved, and is more than a mile in length, is now one of the most noble thoroughfares in the world, being perhaps only surpassed by the BROADWAY OF NEW YORK, to which it has not inaptly been compared."

What wonder was Leith Walk to be compared to the Broadway of New York?

Robert Louis Stevenson, a Scottish novelist, poet and travel writer, most noted for writing Treasure Island, Kidnapped, Strange Case of Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde, and A Child's Garden of Verses, used to spend some of his days at his grandfather's house.

He as well left an invaluable written picture of what Leith Walk used to be: “My grandfather’s house must have been a paradise for boys. It was a great size and had an infinity of cellars below, and of garrets, apple-lofts etc., above, and it had a long garden which ran down to the foot of the Calton Hill. Its front was Leith Walk, with its traffic; at one side was a very deserted lane with the office door and carpenter’s shop and the like: and behind were the big open slops of the Calton Hill”

He loved his Saturday trips to Leith with his grandfather to see the ships; they always passed Mr Smith’s shop at the corner of Union Street and Leith Walk where a model theatre was set up in the window in working order, with sets of a forest, a combat or robbers carousing, and the books of plays – “Penny Plain or Twopence Coloured” tumbled one upon another beside it.

From the pictures, we can see that where it used to be Mr Smith’s shop, now there is a coffee-tea shop, and that the garden at his grandfather's house now it is a hotel.

With the changes, Pilrig free church was opened in 1843 and “in the midst of a growing population rapidly increased” until 1863 a handsome new church was built on the corner of Leith Walk and Pilrig Street, to which Church Halls were added in 1893. Also, at Pilrig a group of Model Building or colony houses were erected in 1851, as an effort to alleviate the housing problems. They are called "Shaw Colonies".

Finally, with the increasing population, legions of the “whores of Leith Walk”, whose activities eventually became intolerable, Edinburgh introduced a new legislation to drove them down past the Boundary Bar into streets which later had to be re-named in order to lose their ill reputation.

Late nineteenth century

After the competing railway companies came to Leith Walk in the middle of the century, large sidings were built and industries appeared nearby which could benefit from them. Many new industrial firms had moved into Leith Walk.

Previous Next

Many new industrial firms had moved into Leith Walk and where Anderson’s Foundry was, now Bertram’s built a paper-making machinery for export all over the world. Croall’s expertly-built carriages were also world famous, and they conducted a large transport and funeral business. Over seven thousand of goods per month we being trans-shipped from Leith Walk Goods Station, one of the biggest in Scotland.

The railway provided work for large number of people, but it can hardly have enhanced the quality of life for those living near it.

The tenements erected on the east side of Leith Walk were neither so grand nor so pleasant as earlier buildings had been.

They were intended for Railway employees and other working people. They were solid buildings with little or no open space around them for recreation.

The horse-drawn buses were followed by horse-trams in the 1870’s, cable cars in 1899 and electric cable cars in 1910.

Below Pilrig, the trams converted to electricity in 1904. They were operated by Leith, and at one time it was necessary to change vehicles there. It was well-known that in addition to fare-paying passengers they carried a great number of fleas.

By the time of the 1891 Census the number of people living in the nine- storey tenements of Greenside Place did not increase, because they were already so overcrowded that no further increase was possible. Fever statistics and Public Health Reports reveal the terrible state of working-class housing.

Greenside was a hotbed of crime, but for nearly a hundred years already one site at the Head of the Walk has been associated with the Theatre.

A cursed theatre

In 1790 the Edinburgh Equestrian Circus was built there, which was distroyed by the fire.

Two years later Stephen Kemble put up a more permanent building which was taken over by Corri. Corri went bankrupt and Henry Siddons refitted the Circus, which later became the Pantheon.

Previous Next

By 1823 it was called the Caledonia Theatre and was described as “dingy, dirty and defectively lighted”. R.H. Wyndham remodeled, decorated and elegantly upholstered it in 1851 and at the request of the Justices of the Peace, Midlothian, obtained a fire certificate signed by architect David Rhind. The fire certificate may have reassured the audience but in May 1853 the Theatre was “reduced to ashes in the brilliant glare of a summer sun”.

After some delay the new Queen’s theatre and Operetta House, designed by David Bryce, was opened in 1855, changed its name to Theatre Royal and in 1864 “was entirely redecorated at great expense, and in a style of chaste elegance which in that respect made it one of the handsomest theaters in the Kingdom”. Within a few months, however, during the run of “Little Tommy Tucker” it burned down again. This time, though the fire occurred late one winter’s afternoon when only one person was inside, and though he was rescued from the roof by a ladder, the gable wall next to St. Mary’s Chapel fell, trapping a man, and in attempting to rescue him the Dean of Guild and five others were killed.

David MacGibbon designed a new building and ten years later it burned down again. C.J. Phipps designed another new building which lasted nine years until yet another fine in 1884.

Nowadays, only St. Mary’s Church stands there. A new mall is under construction behind and John Lewis stands next to where the Royal Theatre used to be.

Twentieth Century

In Greenside, a common privy of iron was still in use and in 1899 an Inquiry was held which resulted in sweeping demolitions there.

In Greenside, The houses which replaced those demolished at that time were never popular and under the 1958 Clearance Order 256 houses, some of which had stood since a very destructive fire in 1836, were knocked down. A plan to build a swimming pool there came to nothing, and now a car-park and the site of a future BBC Television Centre occupy most of the ground where the old village stood. Now, a mall and a office complex stands there.

In 1872, a new school in Lover’s Lane, now Brunswick Road, overlooking the railway, a handsome new school had been built. Designed by Messrs. Moffat & Aitken in the “collegiate gothic style”. It was officially opened on 6th November 1876.

Next to it, there was another school put up by the Original Ragged Schools Association, where boys were being trained in tailoring, shoemaking, carpentry, boxmaking, brace-making and hair-teasing, and girls learnt knitting, sewing and household occupations. Both are still standing.

From 1873, Professor Williamson and forty of the students formed a new college in Gayfield House, which continued until 1904.

Leith Street

In 1911, Walter McPhail described Leith Street as “as busier than ever, with variety, life and light about it, one of the most brilliantly illuminated thoroughfares in any city, and the brightest in Edinburgh”.

There were 22 pubs between Leith Street and York Place and many ice-cream shops. McPhail continues: “In the electric light they flash like ice-bergs in the sunshine. Internally some of them are beautifully firred ip with mahogany and crystal; while the metal-work shines like that on a man of war.” There were dairies with polished zinc counters. LittleJhon’s tearoom was famous for its shortbread. The shop at the top of Letih Street was Edinburgh Rock Shop, and opposite were Ruthersford’s Spanish Lounge.

At the low Calton, the new Black Bull. Above was a Bull’s head with eyes which lit up at night. Two tobacconists’ shops. One was a Highland soldier taking snuff. Further down the hill Mrs Quilletti’s Five Step Saloon was managed by Santos Brattisani, who cooked fish and chips.
